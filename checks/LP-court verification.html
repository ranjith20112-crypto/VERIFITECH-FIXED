We use multi-parameter matching to ensure accurate identification and avoid false positives. Our system cross-references not just the candidate's name, but also father's name, date of birth, address history, and PAN/Aadhaar linkage where available. For common names like "Rajesh Kumar" or "Priya Sharma," we verify additional identifiers such as age range, location proximity, and case filing dates against the candidate's residential history. Each potential match is manually reviewed by legal experts before flagging, ensuring that candidates are not wrongly associated with cases filed by namesakes.

Pending cases are active legal proceedings where the trial is ongoing or judgment is awaitedâ€”these pose immediate hiring risks especially for fiduciary roles. Disposed cases are concluded matters with final judgments (acquittal, conviction, or compromise). We categorize disposed cases as "Acquitted" (cleared of charges) or "Convicted" (found guilty). For BFSI and security-sensitive roles, even acquitted cases may be reviewed depending on the nature of allegations. Our report clearly distinguishes these statuses with next hearing dates for pending cases and final verdict details for disposed matters, helping HR make informed decisions based on your risk appetite.

By default, we focus on cognizable offenses (serious crimes where police can arrest without warrant) as these are recorded in court databases and Crime Record Bureaus. However, we can customize checks to include specific non-cognizable offenses (NC) based on your industry requirements. Traffic violations are generally not checked unless specifically requested for driving-related roles (logistics, transportation). We can verify Motor Vehicle Act violations and driving license suspensions through RTO integration. For senior leadership roles, we recommend comprehensive checks including NCs related to financial misconduct, domestic violence, or moral turpitude even if they don't result in arrest.

While e-Courts integration covers approximately 95% of case data from District Courts and 100% of High/Supreme Court records, some older cases (pre-2010) or recent filings in remote districts may not be digitized. For these, we conduct physical court visits where our field executives manually inspect case registers, cause lists, and judgment copies at the relevant District Court Complex. This includes checking the General Diary (GD) at local police stations for FIRs that may not have reached trial stage. Physical verification adds 3-5 days to the TAT but ensures comprehensive coverage, particularly important for candidates from rural areas or those with older address history.

Absolutely. We follow a "Right to Explain" protocol before finalizing adverse reports. If we discover pending cases or convictions, we share the basic details (case number, court, sections, and status) with the candidate and allow 48-72 hours for explanation. Candidates can provide court-certified copies of acquittal orders, stay orders, or compromise deeds that may not reflect in online databases. Our legal team reviews these documents to update the final report with contextual informationâ€”for example, distinguishing between a malicious 498A case that was quashed versus a genuine financial fraud conviction. This ensures fair evaluation while maintaining screening rigor.

Yes, our court record checks comply with Indian privacy laws and the Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDP). Court records in India are public documents under the Right to Information (RTI) Act, and accessing them for background verification is legal with candidate consent. We obtain explicit written consent before initiating checks, store data on encrypted servers within India, and share reports only with authorized HR personnel. We do not access sealed court records or juvenile justice records (JJ Act protections). For international candidates, we ensure GDPR compliance by limiting data retention to 90 days post-verification unless legally required otherwise.
